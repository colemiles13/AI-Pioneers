Joseph Weizenbaum

The Man Who Taught a Machine to Chat

Learn about the computer scientist who created one of the first chatbots and then became a leading critic of artificial intelligence.

The Creation of ELIZA

In the mid-1960s, Weizenbaum created **ELIZA**, a computer program that could mimic a conversation. It was designed to act like a Rogerian psychotherapist, simply reflecting the user's questions back to them.

ELIZA didn't actually understand what the user was saying. It used simple rules and keyword matching to create its responses, but many people were convinced they were talking to a real, intelligent being.

The ELIZA Effect

Belief in a Machine

Weizenbaum was shocked by how easily people were fooled by ELIZA. Even his own secretary, who knew it was a program, began confiding in it as if it were a real person.

The Dangers of Deception

He realized that people were so eager to believe a computer was intelligent that they would project human feelings and understanding onto a very simple program. He called this phenomenon the **"ELIZA effect."**

A Critical Turn

This experience changed Weizenbaum's views. He went from being a pioneer of AI to a vocal critic, warning about the dangers of blindly trusting technology.

A Call for Responsibility

Weizenbaum's book, *Computer Power and Human Reason*, argued that computers should not be used for tasks that require human qualities like compassion, judgment, and wisdom.

He believed that while machines could be incredibly useful tools, we should never delegate our most important decisions to them. His work helped create the field of **AI ethics**, which is more important now than ever before.
